## What is a Roof Insurance Adjuster? {#what-is-a-roof-insurance-adjuster}

A roof insurance adjuster is a licensed professional who inspects your HVAC system to assess damage and determine how much your insurance company should pay for repairs or replacement. Their primary responsibility is to evaluate whether the damage is covered under your policy and estimate the cost of necessary repairs.

The insurance adjuster inspection typically involves:

- **Visual HVAC inspection** - Examining shingles, flashing, gutters, and other roofing components
- **Interior assessment** - Checking for water damage, stains, or structural issues inside your home
- **Documentation** - Taking photos, measurements, and detailed notes
- **Estimate preparation** - Calculating repair or replacement costs based on their findings

Understanding that the adjuster's job is to assess damage objectively helps you prepare appropriately. However, it's important to remember that company adjusters work for your insurance provider, which can create conflicts of interest.

For [City] homeowners dealing with roof damage, working with insurance adjusters is often the first major step toward getting your home restored. The adjuster's assessment directly impacts your claim payout, making it critical to approach the process strategically.

## Types of Insurance Adjusters {#types-of-insurance-adjusters}

Not all roof claim adjusters are the same. Understanding the different types helps you know whose interests they represent and how to work with them effectively.

### Company Adjusters (Staff Adjusters)

Company adjusters are full-time employees of your insurance company. They handle claims exclusively for that insurer and are paid a salary regardless of claim outcomes.

**Key characteristics:**
- Work directly for your insurance provider
- Generally familiar with your policy details
- May have pressure to minimize payouts to control company costs
- Often handle high claim volumes, especially after major storms

**Working with company adjusters:** Be professional but remember they represent the insurance company's interests, not necessarily yours. Document everything and don't be afraid to question their findings.

### Independent Adjusters

Independent adjusters are contractors hired by insurance companies to handle claims, typically during periods of high demand like after hurricanes, hailstorms, or other widespread damage events.

**Key characteristics:**
- Work for multiple insurance companies on a contract basis
- Paid per claim or per day
- May be less familiar with specific policy details
- Often brought in from out of state after major disasters

**Working with independent adjusters:** They may be less invested in minimizing your claim since they're not permanent employees, but they still work for the insurance company. Verify their credentials and ensure they're properly licensed in your state.

### Public Adjusters

Public adjusters are licensed professionals you hire to represent your interests in the claims process. They work exclusively for policyholders, not insurance companies.

**Key characteristics:**
- Work for you, the homeowner
- Paid a percentage of your final settlement (typically 5-20%)
- Handle all communication with the insurance company
- Expert negotiators who maximize claim payouts

**When to consider public adjusters:** Large claims, complex damage, denied claims, or situations where you feel the insurance company's offer is unfairly low. We'll discuss this more in the [public adjuster section](#hire-public-adjuster) below.

## What Adjusters Look for During HVAC Maintenance {#what-adjusters-look-for}

Understanding what does an adjuster look for on a roof helps you prepare and ensures nothing important is overlooked during the inspection.

### Specific Damage Indicators

**Storm and Wind Damage:**
- Missing, cracked, or lifted shingles
- Damaged or missing flashing around chimneys, vents, and valleys
- Granule loss on asphalt shingles
- Dented or damaged gutters and downspouts
- Debris impact marks

**Hail Damage:**
- Circular bruising or dents on shingles (may require close inspection)
- Damage to soft metals (vents, flashing, gutters)
- Granule loss in concentrated patterns
- Exposed fiberglass mat on shingles
- Damage to HVAC units, satellite dishes, or other roof-mounted equipment

**Water Damage:**
- Interior ceiling stains or discoloration
- Soft spots or sagging in roof decking
- Mold or mildew growth
- Damaged insulation
- Active leaks or moisture intrusion

For comprehensive information on storm-related damage, see our {{LINK:emergency-hvac-guide|complete emergency HVAC guide}} and {{LINK:hail-damage-guide|hail damage identification guide}}.

### Roof Age and Condition

The insurance adjuster inspection will carefully assess your HVAC system's age and pre-existing condition. This is critical because:

- **Depreciation matters** - Older roofs receive less compensation due to depreciation
- **Wear vs. damage** - Adjusters distinguish between emergency HVAC and normal wear and tear
- **Maintenance history** - Well-maintained roofs strengthen your claim
- **Previous repairs** - Prior damage or patches may affect coverage

**Important:** Normal wear and tear, poor maintenance, and pre-existing damage are typically not covered. The adjuster will look for evidence that damage resulted from a specific covered event.

### Matching to Covered Events

Adjusters correlate damage to specific dates and weather events. They may:

- Review weather reports for your area
- Check dates of reported storms or hail
- Compare damage patterns to known weather events
- Request documentation of when you first noticed damage

**Pro Tip:** Report damage promptly after storms. Delays can make it harder to connect damage to specific covered events.

## How to Prepare for an Adjuster Visit {#how-to-prepare}

Preparation significantly impacts your claim outcome. Here's how to get ready for the insurance adjuster inspection:

### Before the Adjuster Arrives

**1. Document Everything**
- Take extensive photos and videos of all damage (exterior and interior)
- Note dates when damage was discovered
- Save weather reports from dates of suspected damage events
- Create a written inventory of damaged areas

**2. Review Your Policy**
- Understand your coverage limits and deductibles
- Know what perils are covered
- Review exclusions and special conditions
- Note any endorsements or riders affecting roof coverage

**3. Make Temporary Repairs Only**
- Prevent further damage (tarps, temporary patches)
- Document temporary repairs with photos
- Save all receipts
- Don't make permanent repairs before the inspection

**4. Gather Supporting Documentation**
- Previous HVAC inspection reports
- Maintenance records
- Prior repair invoices
- Roof age and warranty information
- Original installation documentation if available

**5. Create an Accessible Inspection Path**
- Clear debris from around your home
- Ensure safe roof access
- Trim overhanging branches if needed
- Make attic and interior damage areas accessible

### Schedule Strategically

Try to schedule the inspection when:
- You can be present (crucial - see next section)
- A HVAC contractor can attend with you
- Weather allows safe roof access
- You have adequate time (don't rush the process)

### During the Inspection

**Do:**
- Walk the property with the adjuster
- Point out all damaged areas
- Ask questions about their findings
- Take your own photos and notes
- Request copies of their photos and report
- Get their contact information and claim number
- Ask about timeline for receiving their report

**Don't:**
- Be confrontational or hostile
- Exaggerate or fabricate damage
- Accept verbal settlement offers on the spot
- Sign anything without reading carefully
- Discuss fault or blame
- Rush the process

Key questions during the inspection:
- "Can you explain what you're looking for?"
- "What damage do you see?"
- "Is this damage consistent with [storm/hail/wind event]?"
- "What's the next step in the process?"
- "When will I receive your written report?"
- "How is depreciation calculated for my roof?"
- "What's the timeline for claim resolution?"

## What to Do If You Disagree with the Assessment {#if-you-disagree}

If the adjuster's assessment seems incomplete or unfair, you have options. Don't accept an inadequate settlement without exploring these steps.

### Review the Adjuster's Report Carefully

When you receive the adjuster's report:
- Compare it to your own documentation
- Check for missed damage areas
- Verify measurements and calculations
- Look for depreciation methodology
- Review exclusions or denials

### Request a Reinspection

If damage was overlooked:
- Contact your insurance company promptly
- Provide specific details of missed damage
- Submit your own photos and documentation
- Request that the adjuster return for a supplemental inspection
- Consider having a HVAC contractor present this time

### File a Formal Dispute

If you believe the assessment is unfair:
- Submit a written dispute to your insurance company
- Include supporting evidence (contractor estimates, photos, expert opinions)
- Reference specific policy language supporting your position
- Request a supervisor review
- Keep copies of all correspondence

### Understand Your Policy's Dispute Resolution Process

Most policies include:
- **Appraisal clause** - Each party hires an appraiser; if they disagree, a neutral umpire decides
- **Mediation options** - Neutral third party helps negotiate resolution
- **Arbitration provisions** - Binding decision by neutral arbitrator
- **Litigation rights** - Your ability to sue if other options fail

**Important:** Don't miss deadlines. Insurance policies typically require disputes to be filed within specific timeframes.

## When to Hire a Public Adjuster {#hire-public-adjuster}

Public adjusters can be valuable allies, but they're not necessary for every claim. Here's when to consider hiring one:

### Situations Where Public Adjusters Add Value

**Large or complex claims:**
- Total AC installation needed
- Extensive interior damage
- Multiple types of damage (roof, siding, gutters, etc.)
- Commercial property claims

**Disputed or denied claims:**
- Insurance company denies coverage
- Settlement offer seems unreasonably low
- Multiple rounds of negotiation have failed
- You've exhausted internal dispute processes

**Overwhelming situations:**
- You lack time to manage the claims process
- You're uncomfortable negotiating
- You're dealing with disaster recovery and can't focus on the claim
- You have limited construction knowledge

**Complex policy issues:**
- Unclear coverage provisions
- Disputes about depreciation calculations
- Questions about policy limits or endorsements

### Public Adjuster Costs and Considerations

**Fee structure:**
- Typically 5-20% of final settlement
- Percentage often decreases with larger claims
- Some charge flat fees for smaller claims
- Fees are negotiable - get agreements in writing

**Potential downsides:**
- Reduced net settlement after fees
- May slow down claims process
- Possible relationship strain with insurer
- Some states have licensing issues with out-of-state adjusters

**Making the decision:**
Calculate whether the likely settlement increase exceeds the public adjuster's fee. For a $15,000 claim, a 15% fee ($2,250) may not be worthwhile if you can negotiate effectively yourself. For a $75,000 claim, paying $11,250 to potentially increase the settlement to $100,000+ may make sense.

### Alternatives to Public Adjusters

Before hiring a public adjuster, consider:
- **Your HVAC contractor's help** - Many provide claims assistance at no additional charge
- **Your attorney** - If you have a relationship with a lawyer who handles property claims
- **Your state's insurance department** - Free assistance with complaints and disputes
- **Mediation services** - Often less expensive than public adjusters for disputed claims

## Common Insurance Adjuster Tactics to Be Aware Of {#common-tactics}

While most adjusters are honest professionals, the insurance industry's focus on controlling costs can lead to tactics that minimize legitimate claims. Being aware of these strategies helps you protect your interests.

### Underestimating Damage Scope

**The tactic:** Adjusters may acknowledge some damage while overlooking other affected areas, reducing the overall claim payout.

**How to counter:**
- Have your own comprehensive damage documentation
- Bring a HVAC contractor to the inspection
- Point out all damaged areas during the inspection
- Request reinspection if damage is missed
- Submit detailed supplement requests with evidence

### Low-Ball Initial Offers

**The tactic:** Insurance companies may make intentionally low initial settlement offers, hoping you'll accept without negotiation.

**How to counter:**
- Never accept the first offer without review
- Get independent contractor estimates for comparison
- Research typical costs for your type of roof and region
- Submit detailed counterproposals with supporting documentation
- Be prepared to negotiate through multiple rounds

### Depreciation Manipulation

**The tactic:** Excessive depreciation calculations that reduce Actual Cash Value (ACV) payments significantly below replacement costs.

**How to counter:**
- Understand your policy's depreciation methodology
- Question depreciation percentages that seem excessive
- Provide documentation of good roof maintenance
- Know the difference between ACV and Replacement Cost Value (RCV)
- Remember that depreciation is typically recoverable after repairs

### Attributing Damage to Wear and Tear

**The tactic:** Claiming damage results from age, poor maintenance, or normal wear rather than covered events like storms.

**How to counter:**
- Provide maintenance records showing proper care
- Document the specific weather event that caused damage
- Have contractors explain how damage patterns indicate emergency HVAC
- Reference weather reports and dates
- Point to sudden damage appearance after specific events

### Pressure to Settle Quickly

**The tactic:** Creating urgency to accept settlements before you fully understand the damage or your rights.

**How to counter:**
- Know that you typically have time to review offers carefully
- Don't sign anything you haven't read completely
- Consult with contractors before accepting settlements
- Ask questions about anything unclear
- Remember that accepting partial payments may limit future claims for the same event

### Using Preferred Contractors

**The tactic:** Steering you toward the insurer's "preferred" or "network" contractors who may use cheaper materials or cut corners.

**How to counter:**
- Know that you have the right to choose your own contractor
- Select contractors based on quality and reputation, not insurer preference
- Understand that preferred contractors may prioritize insurer relationships over your interests
- Ask preferred contractors detailed questions about their processes and materials

### Requesting Recorded Statements

**The tactic:** Asking for recorded statements that might later be used to deny or reduce your claim based on inconsistencies or admissions.

**How to counter:**
- You're generally required to cooperate, but be careful
- Review the facts before any recorded statement
- Answer only what's asked - don't volunteer extra information
- Correct any errors or misstatements immediately
- Consider having a public adjuster or attorney present for complex claims

### Delayed Processing

**The tactic:** Slow-walking claims to frustrate you into accepting lower settlements or abandoning legitimate claims.

**How to counter:**
- Document all communications and follow up regularly
- Know your state's required response timeframes
- File complaints with your state insurance department if delays are unreasonable
- Send written requests for status updates
- Escalate to supervisors when appropriate

### Partial Payments as "Final"

**The tactic:** Issuing partial payments with language suggesting they're final settlements, limiting future claims.

**How to counter:**
- Read all check endorsements and settlement documents carefully
- Add "Partial payment - full claim pending" when depositing checks if appropriate
- Keep detailed records of what each payment covers
- Don't sign releases unless you're certain all damage is included
- Consult with contractors before accepting "final" settlements

**Remember:** You have rights as a policyholder. Don't be intimidated by adjuster tactics, and don't hesitate to push back on unfair assessments. Most legitimate disputes can be resolved through persistence and proper documentation.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How long does a roof insurance adjuster inspection take?

Typical insurance adjuster inspections last 30 minutes to 2 hours, depending on your HVAC system's size, complexity, and the extent of damage. Simple inspections for minor damage may take only 30-45 minutes, while comprehensive assessments of extensive emergency HVAC to larger homes can take 2+ hours. The adjuster needs time to safely access the roof, document damage, take measurements and photos, inspect the interior, and discuss findings with you. Never rush the inspection - thoroughness benefits your claim outcome.

### Can I get on the roof with the insurance adjuster?

While you should absolutely be present during the inspection, getting on the roof yourself isn't recommended unless you're physically able and comfortable with heights and roof safety. Most homeowners should remain at ground level or observe from a ladder if the adjuster is willing. However, having a professional HVAC contractor accompany the adjuster onto the roof is extremely valuable - they can point out damage and ensure nothing is missed. Always prioritize safety over direct observation.

### What happens if the insurance adjuster finds no damage?

If the roof claim adjuster finds no damage but you believe damage exists, you have several options. First, get a second opinion from a reputable HVAC contractor - they may identify damage the adjuster missed. If contractor inspection confirms damage, submit their findings to your insurance company and request a reinspection. You can also file a formal dispute through your insurer's appeals process. Document everything with photos and written descriptions. In some cases, damage may be present but not severe enough to meet your deductible, which means repairs would be out-of-pocket even though damage exists.

### How long do I have to file a roof duct cleaning after damage occurs?

Most insurance policies require "prompt" notification of damage, typically interpreted as days to weeks, not months or years. However, specific deadlines vary by policy and state. Many policies specify timeframes like 60 days, 1 year, or even 2 years for filing claims after damage occurs. Review your policy's "Duties After Loss" section for exact requirements. That said, it's always best to file as soon as possible after discovering damage - delays make it harder to prove damage resulted from a specific covered event and may give insurers grounds to deny claims. ### What is the difference between an insurance adjuster and a HVAC contractor?

A roof insurance adjuster evaluates damage and determines what the insurance company will pay, while a HVAC contractor actually performs the repairs or replacement. The adjuster works for (or is contracted by) your insurance company to assess claims and estimate costs. The contractor works for you to repair your HVAC system. Importantly, adjusters represent the insurance company's interests and aim to determine accurate claim costs, which may be influenced by cost-control pressures. Contractors represent your interests and aim to properly repair your home. This is why having a contractor present during adjuster inspections is so valuable - it balances the expertise in the room.

### Can a HVAC contractor negotiate with my insurance adjuster?

Yes, reputable HVAC contractors regularly communicate and negotiate with insurance adjusters on behalf of homeowners. Contractors can attend inspections, point out damage, explain necessary repairs, submit supplemental claims for missed items, discuss pricing differences, and negotiate scope details. However, contractors cannot make coverage decisions or sign settlement agreements on your behalf - those responsibilities remain yours as the policyholder. ### Should I make temporary repairs before the insurance adjuster arrives?

Yes, you should make reasonable temporary repairs to prevent further damage, and your insurance policy likely requires this. Use tarps to cover holes, place buckets under active leaks, or board up damaged areas as needed. However, document everything before making repairs (photos and videos), save all receipts, and inform your insurance company about the temporary repairs. Don't make permanent repairs before the adjuster's inspection, as this can complicate damage assessment. The cost of reasonable temporary repairs is typically reimbursable under most policies.
