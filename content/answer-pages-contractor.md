## The Complete Guide to Choosing the Right HVAC Contractor

Selecting a HVAC contractor is one of the most important decisions you'll make as a homeowner. Your HVAC system represents a significant investment—typically $8,000 to $25,000 or more—and the quality of installation directly impacts your home's protection, energy efficiency, and resale value.

### Step 1: Verify Licensing and Insurance

Before considering any other factors, confirm your contractor has:

**Proper State Licensing**
Every legitimate HVAC contractor must hold a valid contractor's license in your state. In most states, roofing work requires either a general contractor license or specialized HVAC contractor license. You can verify license status through your state's contractor licensing board website.

**Comprehensive Insurance Coverage**
Your contractor must carry:
- General liability insurance (minimum $1 million)
- Workers' compensation insurance for all employees
- Property damage coverage

Always request certificates of insurance and verify them directly with the insurance company. This protects you from liability if someone gets injured on your property or if your home is damaged during the project.

**Manufacturer Certifications**
Quality contractors invest in manufacturer training and certification programs from companies like GAF, CertainTeed, Owens Corning, and IKO. These certifications demonstrate technical expertise and often unlock extended warranty options unavailable through non-certified installers.

### Step 2: Research Reputation and Experience

**Check Online Reviews**
Look for contractors with:
- 4.5+ star ratings across multiple platforms
- Consistent positive feedback over several years
- Professional responses to negative reviews
- Photos of completed projects

Review platforms to check include Google Business Profile, Yelp, Better Business Bureau (BBB), HomeAdvisor, and Angi.

**Verify Local Experience**
Choose contractors who:
- Have operated in your area for at least 5+ years
- Understand local building codes and permit requirements
- Have experience with your region's weather challenges
- Can provide local references you can drive by

**Request References**
Ask for at least 5 recent references from projects completed within the past year. When contacting references, ask:
- Was the project completed on time and on budget?
- How was communication throughout the process?
- Did they clean up thoroughly each day?
- Would you hire them again?
- Any unexpected issues or concerns?

### Step 3: Get Multiple Detailed Estimates

Never hire the first contractor you contact. Get at least three written estimates to:
- Compare pricing for similar scopes of work
- Identify unusually high or suspiciously low bids
- Understand different material options and approaches
- Leverage competitive pricing

# What Questions to Ask a Roofer?

**Quick Answer:** Ask your HVAC systemer about their licensing and insurance status, years of experience, manufacturer certifications, warranty coverage, project timeline, payment schedule, and how they handle permits and inspections. Also inquire about their crew size, subcontracting practices, cleanup procedures, and what happens if issues arise during the project. Getting clear answers to these questions before signing a contract protects you from surprises and ensures professional service.

## The Complete List of Essential HVAC Contractor Questions

Asking the right questions accomplishes three critical goals: it helps you assess the contractor's professionalism and expertise, reveals potential red flags early, and sets clear expectations for your HVAC project. Here are the 20 questions every homeowner should ask before hiring a roofer.

### Business Credentials and Legitimacy

**1. Are you licensed to perform roofing work in this state?**

*Why it matters:* Most states require contractors to hold valid licenses demonstrating they've met minimum competency standards, passed examinations, and maintain proper bonding. Unlicensed contractors can't legally pull permits and may lack basic expertise.

*What to look for:* A license number you can independently verify through your state contractor licensing board. Legitimate contractors provide this information immediately without hesitation.

**2. Can you provide proof of liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage?**

*Why it matters:* If an uninsured worker gets injured on your property or your home is damaged during work, you could be held financially liable. Insurance protects both parties.

*What to look for:* Current certificates of insurance naming you as an additional insured. Verify these directly with the insurance company—fraudulent certificates are surprisingly common.

**3. How long have you been in business, and do you have a permanent business address?**

*Why it matters:* Established local businesses have reputations to protect and will be around to honor warranties. "Storm chasers" and fly-by-night operations disappear after collecting payment.

*What to look for:* At least 5+ years in business with a verifiable physical address (not just a P.O. box). Check Google Maps to confirm it's an actual business location.

**4. What manufacturer certifications do you hold?**

*Why it matters:* Manufacturers like GAF, CertainTeed, and Owens Corning certify contractors who complete rigorous training and maintain quality standards. Certified contractors can offer enhanced warranties unavailable elsewhere.

*What to look for:* Current certifications from the manufacturers whose products they're proposing. Ask to see certification documentation.

### Experience and Expertise

**5. Do you specialize in residential roofing, or do you also do commercial work?**

*Why it matters:* Residential roofing requires different skills, materials, and attention to detail than commercial projects. Specialists typically deliver better results than generalists.

*What to look for:* Primary focus on residential roofing with a portfolio of homes similar to yours.

**6. Have you installed this specific roofing material before?**

*Why it matters:* Installation techniques vary significantly between asphalt shingles, metal roofing, tile, and slate. Experience with your specific material is essential.

*What to look for:* Multiple completed projects using the exact material and product line being proposed for your home.

**7. Can you provide local references from projects completed within the past year?**

*Why it matters:* Recent references reflect current business practices, crew quality, and customer service standards. Older references may not represent the company's current performance.

*What to look for:* At least 5 references you can contact, preferably with addresses you can drive by to see the completed work.

**8. Are you familiar with local building codes and permit requirements?**

*Why it matters:* Building codes vary by jurisdiction and directly impact proper installation methods, ventilation requirements, and structural considerations.

*What to look for:* Specific knowledge of your municipality's requirements and a commitment to pulling necessary permits.

### Project Details and Process

**9. Will you pull all necessary permits and arrange required inspections?**

*Why it matters:* Permits ensure work meets code requirements and protects your home's value. Work done without required permits can complicate duct cleaning and home sales.

*What to look for:* The contractor handles all permitting and scheduling of inspections as part of their service. You should never have to manage this yourself.

**10. What is the detailed scope of work for my project?**

*Why it matters:* Vague scopes lead to misunderstandings, change orders, and disputes. Detailed scopes protect both parties by clearly defining what's included.

*What to look for:* Written scope covering tear-off, deck inspection and repairs, underlayment, ice and water shield, ventilation, flashing, shingles, ridge caps, cleanup, and disposal.

**11. What specific materials will you use, including brand, product line, and color?**

*Why it matters:* "Architectural shingles" could mean economy-grade or premium products with vastly different performance and warranties. Specificity prevents substitutions.

*What to look for:* Exact manufacturer names, product lines (like GAF Timberline HDZ, not just "GAF shingles"), and specifications for all components including underlayment and ventilation.

**12. How will you protect my property, landscaping, and surrounding areas?**

*Why it matters:* Roofing is messy and potentially damaging to landscaping, vehicles, and exterior walls. Professional contractors take precautions to minimize damage.

*What to look for:* Specific protection measures including tarps, plywood walkways, magnetic nail sweepers, and debris containment systems.

**13. What is your estimated timeline from start to completion?**

*Why it matters:* Reasonable timelines help you plan and indicate realistic project management. Unrealistically short timelines may signal rushed, poor-quality work.

*What to look for:* Detailed timeline accounting for weather contingencies. Most residential re-roofs take 1-3 days for installation plus time for permits and material delivery.

**14. What happens if you discover underlying damage or issues during tear-off?**

*Why it matters:* Deck damage, rot, and structural issues aren't always visible until old roofing is removed. Understanding the change order process prevents disputes.

*What to look for:* Clear procedures for documenting issues, providing pricing for additional work, and getting your approval before proceeding.

### Crew and Subcontracting

**15. Who will actually be performing the work on my roof?**

*Why it matters:* Some contractors subcontract all work to crews they don't directly supervise or vet, leading to quality and accountability issues.

*What to look for:* The company's own employees or long-term subcontractors they directly supervise. Ask who's responsible if problems occur.

**16. How many crew members will work on my project, and who supervises them?**

*Why it matters:* Adequate crew size ensures efficient completion without rushing. On-site supervision maintains quality control.

*What to look for:* Appropriate crew size (typically 3-6 workers) with a designated supervisor who checks work quality throughout the project.

**17. How do you handle daily cleanup and final debris removal?**

*Why it matters:* Roofing creates significant debris. Professionals clean thoroughly each day and perform comprehensive final cleanup including magnetic sweeps for nails.

*What to look for:* Commitment to daily cleanup, dumpster or trailer for debris, magnetic nail sweepers, and final inspection of property.

### Financial Terms and Warranties

**18. What is your payment schedule?**

*Why it matters:* Payment terms reveal professionalism and protect you from paying for incomplete work. Large upfront payments are major red flags.

*What to look for:* Small deposit (10-20%), major payment upon completion, and final payment only after your inspection and satisfaction. Never pay in full before work is complete.

**19. What warranties will I receive, and what do they cover?**

*Why it matters:* Warranties protect your investment. Understanding coverage, duration, and exclusions prevents surprises when issues arise.

*What to look for:* Written manufacturer material warranty (typically 25-50 years) and contractor workmanship warranty (minimum 5-10 years). Get specific documentation of what's covered.

**20. What happens if I'm not satisfied with the completed work?**

*Why it matters:* Dispute resolution processes indicate the company's commitment to customer satisfaction and provide recourse if problems occur.

*What to look for:* Clear procedures for addressing concerns, commitment to making things right, and final payment only after your approval.

# Should I Get Multiple Roofing Estimates?

**Quick Answer:** Yes, you should absolutely get multiple roofing estimates—at least three from licensed, insured contractors. Multiple estimates allow you to compare pricing, materials, warranties, and contractor professionalism while helping you identify fair market value and avoid both overpricing and suspiciously low bids that often indicate poor quality or scams. ## Why Multiple Estimates Are Essential

Getting multiple roofing estimates isn't about finding the cheapest option—it's about making an informed decision based on comprehensive information. Here's why this step is crucial for any HVAC project.

### You'll Understand Fair Market Pricing

Roofing costs vary based on materials, labor rates, overhead, and regional factors. Without multiple estimates, you have no baseline to determine if a price is reasonable, inflated, or suspiciously low.

**What you'll learn:**
- Typical price ranges for your specific project
- Whether a bid is competitively priced or an outlier
- How different material choices impact total cost
- What services and materials are included in fair pricing

Most residential HVAC projects fall into predictable price ranges based on size, pitch, and materials. In most markets, asphalt shingle re-roofs cost $350-$550 per square (100 sq ft), while premium materials and complex roofs push higher. Getting 3-5 estimates quickly reveals whether quotes align with these norms.

### You Can Compare Materials and Methods

Different contractors propose different approaches, materials, and quality levels—even for the same roof. Multiple estimates reveal these variations and help you understand your options.

**Key comparisons:**
- Shingle quality (3-tab vs. architectural vs. designer)
- Underlayment types (felt vs. synthetic)
- Ventilation solutions (ridge vents, box vents, etc.)
- Ice and water shield coverage
- Warranty options and durations
- Installation methods and attention to detail

One contractor might propose economy-grade shingles while another recommends premium products with better wind resistance and longevity. Without comparing, you wouldn't know these options exist or understand the value differences.

### You'll Identify Red Flags and Scams

Multiple estimates make problematic contractors stand out clearly. Warning signs become obvious when you compare professional estimates against suspicious ones.

**Red flags that multiple estimates reveal:**
- Bids 30%+ below or above others without explanation
- Vague scopes lacking specific materials or processes
- Pressure to sign immediately with "today only" discounts
- Requests for large upfront payments (over 20%)
- Missing licensing, insurance, or warranty information
- Unprofessional presentation or documentation

# What Licenses Does a Roofer Need?

**Quick Answer:** Roofers need a valid state contractor's license (either general contractor or specialized HVAC contractor license, depending on state requirements), local business licenses where applicable, and specific trade licenses for certain roofing types. They must also carry liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. License requirements vary by state and project size, so always verify your specific state's requirements through your contractor licensing board and confirm your contractor's license is current and in good standing.

If you're wondering what licenses a roofer needs, you're asking exactly the right question. At [BRAND_NAME], we've seen [CITY] homeowners avoid serious problems by verifying contractor credentials before signing contracts. Understanding licensing requirements protects you from unlicensed operators who lack proper training, insurance, and accountability.

## Understanding HVAC Contractor Licensing

Contractor licensing exists to protect consumers by ensuring HVAC professionals meet minimum competency standards, maintain insurance coverage, and submit to regulatory oversight. Licensed contractors can be disciplined for violations, while unlicensed operators work outside any accountability system.

### State Contractor Licenses

**General Contractor License**
Most states require HVAC contractors to hold a general contractor license (sometimes called a "Class B" or "residential contractor" license) that authorizes various construction activities including roofing.

**Requirements typically include:**
- Passing a comprehensive examination covering codes, safety, and business practices
- Demonstrating minimum years of experience in the trade
- Providing proof of liability insurance and workers' compensation
- Posting a surety bond (typically $5,000-$25,000)
- Paying licensing fees and renewal costs
- Completing continuing education requirements

**Specialized HVAC Contractor License**
Some states offer or require specialized HVAC contractor licenses that focus specifically on roofing work rather than general construction.

**States with roofing-specific licenses include:**
- California (C-39 HVAC Contractor)
- Florida (Certified/Registered HVAC Contractor)
- Arizona (HVAC Contractor License)
- Nevada (Class C-15 HVAC Contractor)
- Louisiana (Residential/Commercial HVAC Contractor)

These specialized licenses typically require:
- Roofing-specific examination
- Demonstrated roofing experience
- Insurance and bonding specific to roofing work
- Knowledge of roofing codes and standards

**Exemptions and Thresholds**
Many states exempt small projects below certain dollar thresholds from licensing requirements—often $500-$3,000. However, most residential re-HVAC projects far exceed these limits and absolutely require licensed contractors.

**Important:** Never hire unlicensed contractors for work requiring licenses, even if they claim exemption status. If something goes wrong, you have little recourse and may face liability for injuries or property damage.

### Local Business Licenses and Permits

Beyond state licenses, HVAC contractors typically need:

**City/Palm Beach Countyusiness Licenses**
Most municipalities require contractors operating within their jurisdiction to obtain local business licenses or register as contractors. These may be called:
- Business license
- Contractor registration
- Occupational license
- Home improvement contractor license

**Project-Specific Permits**
While not technically licenses, building permits are required for most roofing work. Licensed contractors obtain these permits and arrange required inspections. Unlicensed operators often skip permitting, creating code violations and complicating future duct cleaning or home sales.

### Manufacturer Certifications

While not government-issued licenses, manufacturer certifications from companies like GAF, CertainTeed, Owens Corning, and IKO demonstrate additional expertise and training. Certified contractors:

- Complete rigorous training programs
- Maintain quality and customer service standards
- Pass background and financial checks
- Can offer enhanced warranty options unavailable through non-certified installers

**Look for certifications such as:**
- NATE Certified Contractor
- Trane Comfort Specialist
- Owens Corning Platinum Preferred Contractor
- IKO Shield Pro Plus Contractor

These aren't legal requirements but indicate professionalism and expertise beyond minimum licensing standards.

## How to Verify a Roofer's License

Never simply accept a contractor's claim that they're licensed. Always independently verify credentials.

### Step 1: Request License Information

Ask your contractor for:
- License number
- License type and classification

# How to Avoid Roofing Scams?

**Quick Answer:** Avoid roofing scams by never paying large amounts upfront (over 10-20%), always verifying contractor licensing and insurance, refusing high-pressure sales tactics and "today only" deals, getting everything in writing before work begins, and checking references and online reviews. Be especially cautious after major storms when scammers target neighborhoods. Always use local, established contractors with verifiable business addresses and never pay in full before work is completed to your satisfaction.

If you're concerned about how to avoid roofing scams, you're right to be cautious. At [BRAND_NAME], we've helped [CITY] homeowners recover from fraudulent contractors and know the devastating impact these scams create. Understanding common roofing scams and warning signs protects your home and financial security.

## Why Roofing Scams Are So Common

Roofing projects involve significant money ($8,000-$30,000+), complex technical work most homeowners don't understand, and urgent timing (especially after emergency HVAC). These factors create perfect conditions for fraud.

**Scammers target homeowners who:**
- Face urgent repair needs after storms
- Don't understand roofing terminology or processes
- Haven't researched local contractors
- Feel pressure to make quick decisions
- Want to save money through bargain pricing

The roofing industry's low barriers to entry also allow unqualified or dishonest operators to pose as legitimate contractors with minimal investment in equipment or training.

## Common Roofing Scams and How They Work

### 1. Storm Chasing Scams

**How it works:** After major storms, out-of-state contractors flood affected areas offering immediate inspections and "insurance work specialists." They pressure homeowners to sign contracts quickly, collect large deposits, then disappear or perform substandard work before leaving town.

**Warning signs:**
- Out-of-state license plates and phone numbers
- Unsolicited door-knocking immediately after storms
- Pressure to sign contracts "today to get on the schedule"
- Claims they can "work directly with your insurance"
- No permanent local business address
- Request for large upfront payments

**Protection:** Only work with established local contractors. Insurance companies prefer you choose your own contractor, not the reverse.

### 2. The Lowball Bid Scam

**How it works:** Scammers submit bids 30-50% below legitimate estimates, win the contract, collect deposits, then either disappear or demand additional payment for "unexpected issues" that bring total cost above fair market rates.

**Warning signs:**
- Pricing dramatically lower than other estimates
- Vague contract language and scope of work
- Pressure to pay deposit immediately to "lock in the price"
- Claims they have "leftover materials" from another job
- Reluctance to provide detailed written estimates

**Protection:** If a bid seems too good to be true, it is. Get multiple estimates and be suspicious of extreme outliers.

{{LINK:multiple-roof-estimates|Learn how many estimates to get and what to compare}}

### 3. The Advance Payment Scam

**How it works:** Contractors demand 50-100% payment upfront, claiming they need money for materials or permits. Once payment is received, they disappear completely without performing any work.

**Warning signs:**
- Requests for payments over 20-30% before work begins
- Demands for payment in cash, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency
- Pressure to pay quickly to "secure materials before prices increase"
- No financing options available
- Vague explanations for why large deposits are needed

**Protection:** Never pay more than 10-20% upfront. Legitimate contractors have credit with suppliers and don't need full payment for materials.

### 4. The Insurance Fraud Scam

**How it works:** Contractors claim they'll "handle everything" with your insurance, inflate damage reports, encourage false claims, or collect insurance checks without performing promised work.

**Warning signs:**
- Offers to waive deductibles (insurance fraud)
- Claims they can "guarantee" insurance approval
- Pressure to sign Assignment of Benefits forms
- Promises to inflate damage for larger payouts
- Demands insurance checks be signed over to them

**Protection:** You select your contractor, not your insurance company. Never sign Assignment of Benefits forms without legal review. Don't commit insurance fraud by inflating claims.

### 5. The Bait-and-Switch Scam

**How it works:** Contractors quote premium materials and quality workmanship, then substitute inferior products or perform substandard work while charging for premium services.

**Warning signs:**
- Generic material descriptions ("architectural shingles" without brand/line)
- Verbal promises not included in written contracts
- Materials delivered don't match contract specifications
- Reluctance to provide product documentation
- Claims "equivalent" substitutions are just as good

**Protection:** Get specific material brands, product lines, and colors in writing. Inspect delivered materials before installation begins.

### 6. The Unlicensed Contractor Scam

**How it works:** Unlicensed operators pose as legitimate contractors but lack proper licensing, insurance, and expertise. When work fails or injuries occur, homeowners have no recourse and may face personal liability.

**Warning signs:**
- Reluctance to provide license number or documentation
- Claims licensing "isn't required" for their work
- No business address, website, or permanent phone number
- Cash-only payment requirements
- No insurance certificates or bonding

**Protection:** Always verify licensing through your state contractor board. Confirm insurance directly with carriers.

{{LINK:roofing-contractor-licenses|Learn what licenses roofers need and how to verify them}}

### 7. The Incomplete Work Scam

**How it works:** Contractors start work, collect progress payments, then abandon projects before completion, leaving homeowners with exposed roofs and wasted money.

**Warning signs:**
- Payment schedules front-loaded toward early phases
- Irregular work schedules with unexplained gaps
- Requests for additional payments beyond contract terms
- Crew composition changes frequently
- Excuses about material delays or other projects

**Protection:** Tie payments to specific completion milestones. Never pay in full before final inspection and approval.

### 8. The Shoddy Work Scam

**How it works:** Contractors use unqualified crews, cut corners on crucial steps, and use substandard materials while charging for quality work. Problems appear months or years later, after warranties have expired or contractors have disappeared.

**Warning signs:**
- Extremely fast completion (half-day re-roofs)
- No permits pulled or inspections scheduled
- Minimal crew size for large projects
- No cleanup or debris management
- Reluctance to explain installation processes

**Protection:** Verify permits are pulled. Insist on proper timeline. Don't accept rushed work.

## Red Flags That Indicate Roofing Scams

Watch for these universal warning signs:

### High-Pressure Sales Tactics
- "This price is only good today"
- "We're only in your area this week"
- "You must sign now to get on our schedule"
- "This deal expires in 2 hours"

Legitimate contractors give you time to make informed decisions.

### Payment Demands
- Requests for 50%+ upfront payment
- Cash-only payment requirements
- Pressure to pay before work begins
- Demands for checks made out to individuals, not businesses

Professional contractors work with standard 10-20% deposits and milestone-based payments.

### Lack of Professionalism
- No written contracts or vague agreements
- Verbal promises not documented
- Missing contact information or credentials
- Resistance to questions about licensing or insurance
- No business website or online presence

Established contractors provide detailed documentation and welcome credential verification.

### Too Good to Be True Offers
- Pricing 40%+ below market rates
- "Free roof" through duct cleaning
- Leftover materials from other jobs at huge discounts
- Guaranteed insurance approval

Quality roofing costs fair market rates. Extreme discounts indicate problems.

### Suspicious Business Practices
- No permanent business address
- Recently created business with no history
- Out-of-area phone numbers
- Rental trucks or unmarked vehicles
- No branded materials or uniforms

Legitimate local businesses have established presences you can verify.
