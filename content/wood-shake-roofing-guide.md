# Wood Shake Roofing Guide: Cedar Shakes, Maintenance & Fire Treatment

Wood shake roofing offers unmatched natural beauty and rustic charm that synthetic materials can't replicate. Despite requiring more maintenance than asphalt shingles, wood shakes remain popular for their distinctive appearance, environmental benefits, and premium aesthetic appeal.

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about wood shake roofing, from material selection to maintenance requirements and fire safety considerations.

## What are Wood Shakes?

### Wood Shakes vs. Wood Shingles

**Wood Shakes**:
- Hand-split or sawn on one side
- Thicker (3/8" to 3/4")
- Textured, rustic appearance
- More irregular and varied
- Better ventilation
- Higher cost
- Longer lifespan

**Wood Shingles**:
- Machine-sawn both sides
- Thinner (3/16" to 5/16")
- Smooth, uniform appearance
- More precise and consistent
- Lower profile
- Less expensive
- Shorter lifespan

**This Guide Focuses on**: Wood shakes (the thicker, split material)

### Types of Wood Shakes

**Cedar Shakes** (Most Common):
- Western Red Cedar (premium choice)
- Eastern White Cedar
- Northern White Cedar
- Yellow Cedar (Alaska)

**Other Woods** (Less Common):
- Redwood (premium, expensive)
- Cypress (good for humid climates)
- Pine (treated, budget option)
- Oak (rare, very durable)

**Why Cedar Dominates**:
- Natural oils resist decay
- Insect resistant
- Dimensionally stable
- Beautiful aging (silvery gray)
- Readily available
- Proven performance

## Wood Shake Grades and Types

### Cedar Shake Grades

**Premium/No. 1 Grade**:
- 100% heartwood
- 100% edge grain
- No defects
- Best durability
- Highest cost
- 30+ year lifespan

**No. 2 Grade**:
- Mix of heartwood and sapwood
- Some flat grain allowed
- Minor defects acceptable
- Good performance
- Moderate cost
- 20-25 year lifespan

**No. 3 Grade** (Utility):
- Contains sapwood
- Defects allowed
- Shorter pieces acceptable
- Budget option
- 15-20 year lifespan
- Often used for starter courses

### Shake Styles

**Hand-Split and Resawn**:
- Split face, sawn back
- Most common type
- Natural texture one side
- Flat back for installation
- Excellent performance

**Tapersawn**:
- Both sides sawn
- Diagonal grain
- Machine-made texture
- More uniform thickness
- Good weather resistance

**Straight-Split** (Barn Shakes):
- Both sides split
- Most rustic appearance
- Thick and irregular
- Premium aesthetic
- Challenging installation

### Standard Sizes

**Length**:
- 18" (most common)
- 24" (premium, larger exposure)
- 32" (rare, very expensive)

**Thickness**:
- Medium: 1/2" to 5/8"
- Heavy: 3/4" to 1-1/4"
- Jumbo: Up to 1-1/2"

**Exposure** (visible portion):
- 18" shakes: 7.5" to 8.5" exposure
- 24" shakes: 10" exposure
- Affects coverage and appearance

## Installation Requirements

### Substrate and Preparation

**Roof Deck**:
- Solid sheathing required
- 5/8" plywood or OSB minimum
- Proper spacing (1/8" gaps)
- Sound, rot-free condition

**Underlayment**:
- Breathable felt paper (Type 15 or 30)
- NOT synthetic waterproof membranes
- Allows wood to breathe
- Prevents moisture trapping

**Spacing**:
- 1/4" to 3/8" between shakes
- Allows expansion/contraction
- Critical for longevity
- Prevents buckling

### Installation Method

**Application**:
- Two nails per shake
- Corrosion-resistant (stainless or hot-dipped galvanized)
- 3/4" to 1" from edges
- 1" to 2" above exposure line

**Coursing**:
- Double starter course
- Offset joints minimum 1-1/2"
- No alignment of joints for three courses
- Prevents water infiltration

**Hip and Ridge**:
- Manufactured hip/ridge shakes
- Or site-fabricated
- Overlapped installation
- Weather-tight assembly

**Valleys**:
- Metal valley flashing
- Copper, stainless, or aluminum
- Minimum 24" wide
- Woven shakes not recommended

### Professional Installation Recommended

**Why**:
- Specialized skills required
- Proper spacing critical
- Difficult for DIY
- Affects longevity significantly
- Warranty requirements

**Cost**: $7-$12 per square foot installed (labor and materials)

## Maintenance Requirements

Wood shakes require more maintenance than asphalt shingles but less than often assumed.

### Regular Maintenance

**Annual Inspection**:
- Check for damaged or missing shakes
- Look for moss/algae growth
- Inspect flashing
- Clear debris
- Examine valleys and ridges

**Debris Removal** (2-4 times yearly):
- Clear leaves and needles
- Remove branches
- Clean valleys
- Prevent moisture retention
- Reduce moss growth

**Gutter Cleaning** (quarterly):
- Remove shake debris
- Ensure proper drainage
- Prevent water backup
- Protect fascia

**Moss and Algae Treatment** (as needed):
- Apply zinc or copper strips
- Use eco-friendly treatments
- Gentle brushing (not pressure washing)
- Prevent growth rather than remove

### Periodic Maintenance

**Every 3-5 Years**:
- Replace damaged shakes
- Re-treat for fire resistance (if required)
- Apply preservatives (optional)
- Professional inspection

**Every 10-15 Years**:
- Consider preservative treatment
- Evaluate overall condition
- Major repairs if needed
- Ridge and hip replacement

### Treatments and Preservatives

**Fire Retardant** (where required):
- Pressure-impregnated at factory
- Or topical application
- Must be renewed (topical)
- Certification maintained

**Wood Preservatives**:
- Extend life 5-10 years
- Natural oils or synthetic
- Applied every 5-10 years
- Professional application recommended

**Algae/Moss Preventatives**:
- Zinc or copper strips at ridge
- Eco-friendly treatments
- Applied every 2-3 years
- Prevents organic growth

### What NOT to Do

**Avoid**:
- Pressure washing (damages wood fibers)
- Oil-based sealers (trap moisture)
- Painting (defeats natural beauty and breathability)
- Ignoring damage (spreads quickly)
- Walking on shakes excessively (can crack)

## Fire Resistance and Safety

### Fire Rating Challenges

**Untreated Cedar**:
- Class C or unrated
- Higher fire risk
- Prohibited in some areas
- Insurance concerns

**The Issue**:
- Wood is combustible
- Ember ignition risk
- Wildfire concerns
- Urban interface restrictions

### Fire Treatment Options

**Pressure-Impregnated Treatment**:
- Factory-applied
- Fire retardant chemicals
- Achieves Class A or B rating
- Permanent (life of shake)
- Cost: Add 30-50% to material cost

**Topical Treatments**:
- Applied to installed shakes
- Achieves Class A or B rating
- Requires renewal (5-10 years)
- Less expensive initially
- Ongoing maintenance cost

**UL 2218 Impact Rating**:
- Some treated shakes achieve Class 4
- Insurance discounts possible
- Hail resistance certification

### Building Code Requirements

**Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) Areas**:
- Class A rating often required
- Some areas prohibit wood entirely
- Check local fire codes
- HOA restrictions common

**General Requirements**:
- Varies by jurisdiction
- Insurance company requirements
- May require treatment
- Verify before installation

**Always Check**:
- Local building codes
- Fire district regulations
- HOA rules
- Insurance requirements

### Insurance Considerations

**Untreated Wood Shakes**:
- Higher premiums (20-30% increase)
- Some insurers won't cover
- Deductible requirements
- Limited coverage options

**Treated Wood Shakes**:
- Class A: Same rates as asphalt
- Discounts sometimes available
- Broader insurer options
- Proof of treatment required

**Maintain Documentation**:
- Treatment certificates
- Renewal records
- Inspection reports
- For insurance and sale

## Costs

### Material Costs

**Cedar Shakes** (materials only):
- No. 1 Grade: $400-$600 per square (100 sq ft)
- No. 2 Grade: $250-$400 per square
- No. 3 Grade: $150-$250 per square

**Fire-Treated Cedar**:
- Add 30-50% to above costs
- Premium for Class A rating

**Other Materials**:
- Underlayment: $30-$50 per square
- Nails: $20-$40 per square
- Flashing: Varies by application
- Ridge/hip shakes: $100-$200 per square

### Installation Costs

**Labor**:
- $3-$7 per square foot
- Varies by region and complexity
- Steep roofs cost more
- Complex designs increase cost

**Total Installed Cost** (typical home):
- Budget (No. 2 grade): $9,000-$15,000
- Standard (No. 1 grade): $12,000-$22,000
- Premium (treated, complex): $18,000-$35,000

**Compared to Asphalt**:
- 2-3 times more expensive initially
- But lasts longer with maintenance
- Total cost of ownership comparable

### Long-Term Costs

**Maintenance**:
- Annual: $200-$500 (debris removal, inspection)
- Periodic repairs: $500-$1,500 every 5 years
- Treatment renewal: $1,000-$3,000 every 10 years

**Total Cost of Ownership** (30 years):
- Initial installation: $20,000
- Maintenance: $9,000
- Total: $29,000
- Cost per year: $967

**Compare to Asphalt**:
- Initial: $12,000
- Replacement (year 20): $15,000
- Total 30 years: $27,000
- Comparable long-term cost

## Lifespan and Durability

### Expected Lifespan

**Untreated Cedar Shakes**:
- No. 1 Grade: 30-40 years
- No. 2 Grade: 20-30 years
- With good maintenance: Up to 50 years

**Fire-Treated Shakes**:
- 20-30 years typical
- Chemicals can reduce lifespan
- Depends on treatment quality
- Maintenance critical

**Factors Affecting Lifespan**:
- Climate (dry climates: longer life)
- Maintenance quality
- Installation quality
- Shade (too much reduces life)
- Ventilation
- Wood grade

### Climate Considerations

**Best Climates**:
- Moderate humidity
- Distinct seasons
- Not constant wetness
- Good air circulation

**Challenging Climates**:
- Constant high humidity
- Prolonged wet seasons
- Extreme heat
- Heavy moss/algae growth areas

**Worst for Wood Shakes**:
- Pacific Northwest (constant moisture)
- Deep South (humidity, termites)
- Desert Southwest (extreme heat dries wood)

**Best Performance**:
- Northeast (moderate, seasonal)
- Mountain regions (dry, cool)
- Upper Midwest (dry, good ventilation)
